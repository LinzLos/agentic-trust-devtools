Designer's cut · the opener
Decision
Open with the trend, not the product. Three rungs of one move so the skip button visibly degrades: decline → can't skip → weren't there.
Why
The horizontal POV has to land before the dev-tools example, or the example looks niche.
Trade-off
Low-fi on purpose — legibility over polish. A clean trend line reads in five seconds.

Designer's cut · the hero
Decision
This beat is the fix: declarative UX. Your standing declaration governs the ranking, and every control shows its consequence on the spot.
Why
The broken pattern already landed in beat 1, so restaging it here is redundant. Pay-to-rank carries the before/after in one view: flip it and the paid option jumps the line; flip it back and best-fit wins.
Trade-off
Not hidden, not banned: sponsorship is surfaced, demoted by your rule, rerouted to your terms. The tolerable middle.

Designer's cut · the audit
Decision
Render allegiance as an audit trail, not a setting — a log you can check, with who-served + why on every row.
Why
"Held, not blocked" only earns trust if the held thing is visible and retrievable.
Trade-off
A queue is a place to maintain — worth it: it turns "the agent screened ads" into something auditable in three seconds.
the pattern → the fix

What I'm describing doesn't exist today.
But every precondition has shipped.

Ads don't die when you remove them. They climb a layer, into the agent's choices. The fix isn't a “Sponsored” label. It's allegiance: the agent works for you. It screens the injected offer and reroutes it to your terms. You declare the rule once, and the agent holds the line.

That's the horizontal pattern. Dev tools is where I showed it. The skip button can come back, at the agent layer.
